The 4 Phases of the Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a complex, delicate interplay of hormones designed to prepare the body for pregnancy. A typical cycle lasts 28 days but can range from 21 to 35 days.

1. Menstrual Phase (Days 1-5)

This is when your period occurs. The thickened uterine lining sheds if pregnancy has not occurred. Estrogen and progesterone levels drop. You might feel fatigued and experience cramps.

2. Follicular Phase (Days 1-13)

Overlapping with your period, the pituitary gland releases FSH. This stimulates ovaries to produce follicles. Estrogen rises, which thickens the uterine lining. You usually feel more energetic.

3. Ovulation Phase (Day 14)

A surge in Luteinizing Hormone (LH) causes the dominant follicle to release a mature egg. This is your most fertile window. You may notice a slight rise in basal body temperature.

4. Luteal Phase (Days 15-28)

After releasing the egg, the follicle becomes the corpus luteum, pumping out progesterone. If unfertilized, hormones plunge, causing PMS symptoms, and a new cycle begins.

Did You Know? Fascinating Period Facts

  • You don't lose as much blood as you think: The average blood loss during a period is only about 2 to 3 tablespoons (30-40ml), though it can look like more!
  • Periods can affect your voice: Hormonal shifts can slightly alter the vocal cords. Female singers sometimes notice less vocal control in the days right before menstruation.
  • Synchronized cycles are a myth: The idea that women living together get their periods at the same time (the McClintock effect) has been debunked by modern research. It's just statistical overlap!
  • Cold weather can make it worse: Winter months can lead to longer, heavier, and more painful periods due to less sunlight and lower vitamin D levels, which affects mood and hormones.
  • You are born with all your eggs: A female fetus has around 6 to 7 million eggs. By puberty, about 300,000 remain, and only about 300 to 400 are ever ovulated during a lifetime.

Understanding PCOS and PCOD

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is an endocrine and metabolic disorder that is more severe. Polycystic Ovarian Disease (PCOD) is a condition where ovaries produce many immature eggs which turn into cysts over time.

Detailed Symptoms:

  • Irregular or completely absent periods
  • Unexplained weight gain or difficulty losing weight
  • Hirsutism (excess facial and body hair)
  • Severe hormonal acne or oily skin
  • Thinning hair on the scalp
  • Insulin resistance (darkening of skin around the neck)

Long-Term Risks & Management:

If left unmanaged, PCOS can lead to type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, and endometrial cancer. However, it is highly manageable. Key strategies include maintaining a low-glycemic diet, engaging in regular resistance training, and managing stress. Supplements like Myo-inositol are often recommended by doctors.

Navigating Menopause

Menopause is a natural biological milestone marking the permanent end of menstrual cycles, officially diagnosed after 12 consecutive months without a period. It usually occurs between ages 45 and 55.

The Three Stages:

  • Perimenopause: The transition phase leading up to menopause. It can start 8 to 10 years before menopause. Hormones fluctuate wildly, causing irregular periods.
  • Menopause: The exact point in time when you have gone a full year without a period.
  • Postmenopause: The years following menopause. Symptoms may ease, but lower estrogen increases the risk for osteoporosis and heart disease.

Management Tips:

Staying active with weight-bearing exercises protects bone density. Eating a diet rich in calcium and vitamin D is crucial. Dressing in breathable layers helps manage hot flashes. Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) may be an option for severe symptoms, but requires a doctor's consultation.

Endometriosis & Uterine Fibroids

Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows outside it, causing severe pain and potential fertility issues. Uterine Fibroids are noncancerous growths of the uterus that often appear during childbearing years.

Common Symptoms:

  • Painful periods (dysmenorrhea) and pelvic pain
  • Pain during or after intercourse
  • Pain with bowel movements or urination
  • Excessive bleeding or bleeding between periods
  • Infertility in some cases of endometriosis

When to See a Doctor:

If you experience pelvic pain that interferes with your daily life, or if your periods have become significantly heavier or more painful, consult a gynaecologist. Early diagnosis is key to managing symptoms and preventing complications.

Breast Health & Awareness

Breast health is a vital part of overall wellness. Being "breast aware" means knowing how your breasts normally look and feel so you can notice any changes early.

Self-Examination Tips (Monthly):

  • Look: In front of a mirror, check for changes in size, shape, or skin texture.
  • Feel: Use the pads of your fingers in a circular motion to check for lumps or thickening.
  • Coverage: Ensure you check the entire breast area, including up to the collarbone and into the armpits.

Warning Signs:

Look out for new lumps, persistent pain, skin dimpling (like an orange peel), nipple discharge, or changes in the nipple (like pulling inward). Most lumps are not cancerous, but all should be checked by a professional immediately.
